Part One

The Root Question:
Why Did This Happen?

The immediate cause of division followed the death of the Prophet Muhammad in 632 CE. The Quran had been revealed, the community established — but no explicit, codified procedure for political succession had been fixed. The question was devastatingly simple yet cosmically consequential:

Who should lead the Muslim community after Muhammad?

The single unanswered question from which everything else grew

Two answers emerged — irreconcilable in their logic, and both deeply rooted in the events of the Prophet's life:

Sunni Answer

Communal Consensus (Shura)

The community's senior companions (Sahaba) should choose the best-qualified person through consultation — a human, political process grounded in tribal tradition and pragmatic governance. The Prophet himself, Sunnis argue, would have made succession explicit if God intended a specific heir.

Shia Answer

Divine Designation (Nass)

God had already designated Ali ibn Abi Talib — the Prophet's cousin and son-in-law — through explicit prophetic declaration at Ghadir Khumm. Leadership is a divine appointment, not a human election. A community choosing its own spiritual leader is as absurd as one choosing its own prophet.

Historical context: The Arabian Peninsula of 632 CE was not a stable, unified state. It was a collection of tribes, clans, and newly converted communities held together primarily by the living authority of Muhammad. His death created a vacuum that was genuinely unprecedented — no governance model existed for a theocratic community without a prophet.

Everything that followed — every battle, every theological argument, every Muharram procession, every difference in prayer — flows from that original disagreement. It is not merely a political dispute. It became, after Karbala, a question of cosmic justice, divine authority, and the very soul of Islam.

The Events,
Step by Step

Every claim below is traceable to named primary sources. Where the source itself is contested, both readings are given. Widely Accepted Scholarly Debate Shia Reading Sunni Reading

1

632 CE — Medina, Arabia

The Saqifa Meeting Disputed Context

While Ali ibn Abi Talib and the Prophet's family were preparing Muhammad's body for burial, Medinan and Meccan Muslim leaders gathered at the covered assembly hall (Saqifat Banu Sa'ida) of the Banu Sa'ida tribe. Abu Bakr, Umar ibn al-Khattab, and Abu Ubayda argued the Quraysh tribe must lead — the Arabs would accept nothing less. Umar pledged allegiance to Abu Bakr on the spot. The first Caliph was established before the Prophet was buried.

Primary Source: Widely Accepted Umar himself later called the Saqifa selection a falta — literally "a rash, impromptu, ill-considered act." This word is preserved in Sahih al-Bukhari, the most authoritative Sunni hadith collection. That is not the language of an orderly, divinely sanctioned transition.

Sahih al-Bukhari al-Tabari, Tarikh

Sunni Reading

Quick, experienced action by senior companions prevented tribal fragmentation at Islam's most vulnerable moment. Ali eventually cooperated with the caliphate as legal counsellor. The Ansar (Medinan supporters) had initially attempted to install one of their own — Abu Bakr's intervention preserved unity.

Shia Reading

Ali was deliberately bypassed while he was occupied with the Prophet's burial — one of the most sacred duties in Islam. Succession was seized, not conferred. The Prophet had already designated his successor at Ghadir Khumm just 80 days earlier, a declaration both factions know occurred.

2

632 CE — Medina

The Standoff at Fatima's House Disputed Details

Abu Bakr's administration dispatched Umar ibn al-Khattab to secure Ali's allegiance. Ali had gathered with Banu Hashim at the home of Fatima al-Zahra, the Prophet's beloved daughter. Historical sources — including Sunni ones — record that Umar threatened to set the house ablaze if they did not emerge and pledge loyalty.

Morally devastating historical fact: Widely Accepted According to Sahih al-Bukhari, Ali withheld his allegiance for a full six months — only pledging after Fatima died. Fatima died estranged from Abu Bakr, having refused his presence at her funeral, over a dispute regarding the inheritance of the Fadak orchard (which she claimed the Prophet had gifted her, and which Abu Bakr refused to transfer, citing a hadith that "prophets leave no inheritance").

Sahih al-Bukhari, Vol. 5 Sahih Muslim
Significance: The Prophet's only surviving daughter died in grief, estranged from the ruling government, and barred the first Caliph from her funeral prayers. This single fact haunts the Sunni narrative. It is not disputed. It is simply not widely discussed.
3

632–656 CE — Arabia, Persia, Egypt

The First Three Caliphs Widely Accepted

Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (632–634 CE): Suppressed the Ridda (apostasy) wars — tribal rebellions that threatened to fracture the young Muslim state. Consolidated Arabia. Initiated the first compilation of the Quran. Died of illness after 27 months in office.

Umar ibn al-Khattab (634–644 CE): The great military administrator. Within a single decade, the caliphate conquered Persia, Egypt, the Levant, Iraq, and parts of North Africa — making Islam a world civilisation. Developed the Diwan (state pension system), established Islamic jurisprudence, and regularised the Islamic calendar. Assassinated by Abu Lu'lu'a, a Persian slave.

Uthman ibn Affan (644–656 CE): Produced the authoritative, standardised Quran — the text used globally today. However, his decade-long practice of appointing Umayyad kinsmen to key governorships bred deep resentment across Egypt, Basra, and Kufa. In 656 CE, rebels besieged his home in Medina and assassinated him while he was reading the Quran. This killing would poison Muslim politics for centuries.

Ali's position across 24 years: He served as chief legal consultant and religious authority under all three Caliphs — never openly rebelling, never accepting their succession as divinely legitimate, but also never taking up arms against them. His deep theological and legal contributions during this period are universally respected by both traditions. al-Tabari
4

656–661 CE — Basra, Siffin, Kufa

Ali's Caliphate & the First Civil War (Fitna) Widely Accepted

After Uthman's assassination, Ali ibn Abi Talib was acclaimed Caliph — finally, after 24 years. But his refusal to immediately prosecute Uthman's killers (many of whom were in his own army and support base) cost him dearly in legitimacy.

Battle of the Camel (Basra, December 656 CE): Aisha — the Prophet's widow and one of his most influential wives — alongside Talha ibn Ubaydullah and al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam marched against Ali, demanding justice for Uthman. This was the first organised battle between Muslims. Ali won. Talha and al-Zubayr were killed in battle. Aisha was sent home to Medina with full honour and dignity.

Battle of Siffin (July 657 CE): Muawiya ibn Abi Sufyan, governor of Syria and Uthman's kinsman, refused to recognise Ali. When Ali's army was on the verge of decisive victory, Muawiya's troops improvised: they mounted pages of the Quran on their spears, demanding arbitration rather than continued fighting. Ali accepted. A faction of Ali's own army — the Kharijites ("those who went out") — declared this compromise apostasy: "Al-hukm lillah" (Judgment belongs only to God). They subsequently assassinated Ali in the mosque of Kufa in 661 CE.

What this means: Ali was killed by his own former supporters — not by Muawiya. The arbitration process ultimately went against Ali, gifting Muawiya the caliphate. His death left the question of succession perpetually unresolved in Muslim political thought. al-Tabari, Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk
5

661 CE — Kufa, Iraq

Hasan's Peace Treaty — and Its Betrayal Widely Accepted

Hasan ibn Ali, the Prophet's elder grandson and 2nd Shia Imam, briefly became Caliph after Ali's assassination. But his coalition was fragmented and financially compromised — Muawiya had been systematically bribing Hasan's commanders. He entered into a negotiated treaty with Muawiya ibn Abi Sufyan, ceding political power in exchange for explicit guarantees.

The Treaty Conditions: (1) No hereditary succession — Muawiya could not name his own son as successor, leaving the matter to community consultation. (2) Full amnesty for Ali's followers — no reprisals. (3) No public cursing or abusing of Ali ibn Abi Talib from mosque pulpits.
What happened next: Widely Accepted Upon entering Kufa in triumph, Muawiya publicly announced — in his own words, preserved by al-Tabari — that he was trampling every condition of the treaty underfoot. He then proceeded to name his son Yazid I as successor, converting the caliphate into a hereditary monarchy in direct violation of Condition 1. He also instituted the public cursing of Ali from mosque pulpits — a practice that lasted under the Umayyads for nearly 60 years until Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz abolished it.

al-Tabari, Vol. 18 al-Baladhuri, Ansab al-Ashraf
6

680 CE — Mecca, Medina

Husayn's Refusal — The Road to Karbala Widely Accepted

When Muawiya died in April 680 CE, his son Yazid I became Islam's first hereditary Caliph. The demand that all leading Muslims pledge allegiance (bay'a) to Yazid arrived swiftly to Husayn ibn Ali in Medina.

Husayn ibn Ali — the Prophet's younger grandson, son of Ali and Fatima, the last living direct link to the Prophet — refused. Not simply as a political act, but as a moral one. Yazid was known publicly for drinking wine, keeping hunting dogs, and openly flouting Islamic conduct — none of which befitted the Caliph of Islam. To recognise Yazid, Husayn believed, was to ratify the corruption of Islam at its institutional core.

The Letters from Kufa: Between 10,000 and 12,000 letters arrived from the people of Kufa, Iraq — the same city that had supported Ali — pledging their support if Husayn came to lead them. His cousin Muslim ibn Aqeel was sent ahead to verify the support. His initial reports confirmed overwhelming popular enthusiasm. Then the Umayyad governor Ubaydallah ibn Ziyad systematically crushed the movement before Husayn could arrive.

On 9 September 680 CE, Husayn departed Mecca with his family — approximately 72 fighters and a larger group of women and children — toward Kufa. He was walking into what would become the most consequential tragedy in Islamic history. Several companions and family members begged him not to go. He refused to be deterred.

al-Tabari, Tarikh Ibn al-Athir, al-Kamil

What Happened — Widely Accepted

The Battle of Karbala

On 2 October, Husayn's caravan reached the desert plain of Karbala, near the Euphrates in modern-day Iraq — roughly 55 miles southwest of Baghdad. Ubaydallah ibn Ziyad, the Umayyad governor of Kufa — the same city that had sent thousands of letters of support — dispatched an army under Umar ibn Sa'd. What had promised to be mass popular support became mass abandonment.

By 7 Muharram, 4,000+ Umayyad soldiers surrounded the camp and severed access to the Euphrates River. For three days, in the blazing October desert heat, Husayn's group — including women, children, and infants — had no water. The psychological warfare of thirst before battle is one of the most viscerally remembered details in Islamic tradition.

On the morning of 10 Muharram (Ashura), Husayn mounted his horse and addressed the enemy army. Historical sources record his final sermon: he offered to leave, return to Medina, or go to the frontier — anything to avoid bloodshed. Ibn Ziyad's orders were absolute: pledge allegiance to Yazid, or die. Husayn chose death.

The battle was brief and utterly one-sided. Husayn's 72 fighters were killed one by one throughout the morning. His half-brother Abbas ibn Ali — who had attempted to bring water from the Euphrates — was ambushed and killed. His infant son Ali al-Asghar was killed in his arms when Husayn held him up and asked for water for the child — an arrow pierced the infant's throat. Husayn himself was killed, beheaded, and his head was mounted on a lance and paraded first to Kufa, then to Damascus — to the court of Yazid.

The women and children — including Husayn's sister Zaynab bint Ali and his surviving son Ali ibn Husayn (Zayn al-Abidin), who was too ill to fight — were taken captive and marched to Kufa, then to Damascus.

Zaynab's Speech in Yazid's Court: When the captives were brought before Yazid in Damascus, Husayn's sister Zaynab bint Ali delivered a defiant and devastating speech. She declared Husayn's death a moral victory, predicted Yazid's downfall, and refused to express grief as submission. Her speech before the caliph who had just massacred her family is one of the most celebrated acts of courage in the entire Islamic tradition — and the primary vehicle through which the story of Karbala was carried to the wider Muslim world.
One survivor of the male line: Zayn al-Abidin (Ali ibn Husayn), too gravely ill to take the field, was spared by the intervention of Husayn's sister Zaynab. He became the 4th Imam of the Shia tradition and preserved the Prophetic lineage. Without him, the Shia imamate would have ended at Karbala. His collection of prayers — the Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya — is considered one of the most beautiful texts in Islamic literature.
What It Became

From Politics to Theology

Before Karbala, the Shia were a political faction — the Shi'at Ali, the "party of Ali" — who believed the caliphate had been wrongly taken from his family. After Karbala, something far more profound crystallised.

Historian Heinz Halm writes in Shi'a Islam (2004): "There was no religious aspect to Shi'ism prior to 680. The death of the third Imam and his followers marked the 'big bang' that created the rapidly expanding cosmos of Shi'ism and brought it into motion."

Heinz Halm, Shi'a Islam (2004)

The theology of redemptive suffering — the idea that Husayn died for the sins of a community that failed him, that his martyrdom carries salvific weight, that weeping for him is itself an act of proximity to God — these concepts emerged in the decades after Karbala, shaped by the very human devastation of what had occurred.

Wilferd Madelung, in The Succession to Muhammad (1997), argues that Karbala permanently changed the moral vocabulary of Islam: "The tragic death of Husayn became the founding event of a theology of martyrdom that reshaped how both traditions understood the relationship between political authority and divine justice."

Wilferd Madelung, The Succession to Muhammad (1997)

Key Theological Evidence
& Primary Sources

The Sunni–Shia debate is not one of faith versus fabrication. Both sides cite real, documented, primary-source evidence. Here are the central texts and their competing interpretations.

مَنْ كُنْتُ مَوْلَاهُ فَعَلِيٌّ مَوْلَاهُ

"Of whomsoever I am the mawla, Ali is also his mawla."

Prophet Muhammad — Ghadir Khumm, 18 Dhul-Hijja, 10 AH (March 632 CE)

Widely Accepted Status: Mutawatir — confirmed by so many independent chains of transmission that both Sunni and Shia scholars agree it cannot be denied. Recorded in Musnad Ahmad, Sunan al-Tirmidhi, and dozens of other collections. The event occurred just 80 days before the Prophet's death — making it one of his final public acts.

Sunni Reading Interpretation

The word mawla means "friend," "ally," or "beloved companion." The occasion was to defend Ali's honour following criticism from his expedition to Yemen. The declaration was a public affirmation of friendship and respect — not an act of political succession. If it were a succession announcement, the companions present would not have needed to choose at Saqifa.

Shia Reading Interpretation

The word mawla clearly means "master" or "one with authority." Context: the Prophet first asked "Do I not have more authority over you than your own souls?" — then declared Ali the mawla. This sequential logic implies a transfer of authority (wilayah), not merely affection. Quran 5:67 — "O Messenger, convey what has been revealed to you from your Lord" — compelled the delivery of this message.

The Three Core Hadiths

Evidence 1 — Both Accept This

Hadith al-Thaqalayn — "The Two Weighty Things"

At multiple occasions, including Ghadir Khumm, the Prophet declared: "I am leaving among you two weighty things: the Book of Allah and my family (Ahl al-Bayt). If you hold fast to both, you will never go astray."

Sunni Reading

A moral reminder to honour the Quran and respect the Prophet's family as a spiritual guide. The hadith does not imply political succession by the Ahl al-Bayt. Some Sunni versions read "my Sunnah" instead of "my family" — pointing to divergent transmission chains.

Shia Reading

An explicit command: just as the Quran is obligatory to follow, so is the Ahl al-Bayt. Both are inseparable guides for the community — removing one invalidates guidance entirely. The pairing is deliberately equal, not hierarchical.

Sahih Muslim Musnad Ahmad Sunan al-Tirmidhi
Evidence 2 — Both Accept This

Hadith al-Manzila — "You Are to Me as Aaron Was to Moses"

At the campaign of Tabuk (630 CE), the Prophet told Ali: "Are you not pleased that you are to me as Aaron was to Moses — except there shall be no prophet after me?"

Sunni Reading

A high honour — deputyship while the Prophet was away at Tabuk. But Aaron historically predeceased Moses, meaning Aaron never led the community after Moses. The hadith therefore refers to temporary deputyship, not permanent succession. Its occasion was specific.

Shia Reading

The entire point of the Aaron-Moses analogy is that Aaron was the designated successor in all things. "No prophet after me" simply clarifies Ali cannot be a prophet — but he can and should be the Imam and rightful leader. The exception proves the rule: he has Aaron's entire station, minus prophethood.

Sahih al-Bukhari, Vol. 5 Sahih Muslim
Evidence 3 — The Contested Incident

The "Thursday Tragedy" — The Prophet's Deathbed Request Disputed Interpretation

In his final illness, the Prophet reportedly asked for writing materials: "Bring me writing materials so that I may write for you something after which you will not go astray." Umar ibn al-Khattab objected: "The man is overcome by fever. We have the Book of Allah — it is enough for us."

Shia interpretation: The Prophet intended to write Ali's name explicitly as successor. Umar deliberately blocked it — the "Thursday Tragedy" (Musibat al-Khamis). The Prophet died without writing what he intended. This is the original moment of usurpation. Ibn Abbas, who narrated this hadith, reportedly wept and called it the "greatest disaster."
Sunni interpretation: Umar was acting from pious concern — the Prophet was clearly gravely ill, and a written command under such conditions might cause confusion or legal complications. Ibn Abbas's grief was for the loss of written guidance, not evidence of conspiracy. Umar's objection was sincere theological caution.
Sahih al-Bukhari, Vol. 1 Sahih Muslim, Vol. 3

Part Five

Muharram:
The Divergence in Practice

The first month of the Islamic calendar — Muharram — has become the single most visible marker separating the two traditions in daily religious life. The same month. The same event. Radically different responses.

Sunni Observance

Ashura as Sacred Fast

For Sunnis, the Day of Ashura (10 Muharram) primarily commemorates the day Moses and his people were saved from Pharaoh, for which the Prophet prescribed fasting on the 9th and 10th of Muharram. Karbala is remembered as a profound tragedy — Husayn is fully honoured as a martyr and beloved grandson of the Prophet — but Ashura's primary religious meaning predates Karbala by over a thousand years of Jewish and early Muslim tradition.


Sahih al-Bukhari Sahih Muslim

Shia Observance

Ashura as Day of Mourning

Ashura is the holiest and most sorrowful day of the Islamic year. Fasting is considered haram — it would signal celebration on the day of the greatest tragedy. The entire ten days of Muharram are a period of intense communal grief: black clothing, the suspension of festive activities, and the ritual remembrance of Husayn's suffering and sacrifice. The intensity of mourning is itself a theological statement about which history matters.

Shia Muharram Practices — Origins & Meaning

Majalis (Gatherings of Lamentation): Communal mourning gatherings where rawdakhwan (lamenters) recite the narrative of Karbala — the suffering of Husayn, his family, and his companions — with the explicit goal of moving listeners to tears. The Arabic and Persian elegy tradition (marsiya) reached extraordinary literary heights through these gatherings. The act of weeping is itself considered a devotional act drawing the mourner closer to God through shared grief with the Prophet's family.
Ta'ziya / Passion Plays (shabih): Dramatic reenactments of the Battle of Karbala performed across Iraq and Iran. The theatrical tradition is one of the oldest continuous theatrical forms in the world — each year reliving the events of 680 CE to maintain emotional and communal connection to the tragedy. European scholars who observed these plays in the 19th century compared them to Greek tragedy and medieval Passion plays.
Ziyara (Pilgrimage to Shrines): Millions of Shia Muslims travel to the shrine of Imam Husayn in Karbala, Iraq — especially on Ashura and Arba'een (40 days later). The Arba'een pilgrimage today draws an estimated 20–25 million people, making it the world's largest annual human gathering, surpassing even the Hajj in numbers.
The Umayyad Politicisation — Evidence of Power Weaponising Religion: Widely Accepted Under Caliph Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan (685–705 CE), the Umayyad dynasty declared Ashura a festive public holiday — the precise opposite of Shia mourning. The goal was explicit: to counter and suppress Shia commemoration of Husayn's death with state-mandated celebration. This is one of the most historically documented examples of political power directly weaponising religious observance to counter opposition.

al-Baladhuri, Ansab al-Ashraf al-Tabari

Part Six

Differences in
Namaz (Prayer)

The differences in prayer flow directly from which chain of religious authority each group trusts. Shia Muslims follow the Jafari madhab (named after Imam Jafar al-Sadiq, the 6th Imam, d. 765 CE) — the jurisprudential school developed by the Shia Imams. Sunnis follow one of four classical schools: Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, or Hanbali.

These are not arbitrary differences. Each stems from a specific hadith, Quranic verse, or chain of authority — and often from a deliberate rejection of a practice the other group introduced after the Prophet's death.

Sunni prayer follows one of four schools (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali). Differences between these schools are minor; the steps below represent the common structure. Tap any step for full detail.

Shia prayer follows the Jafari school, based on teachings of Imam Jafar al-Sadiq (d. 765 CE). Key structural differences from Sunni practice are noted at each step. Tap any step for full detail.

Complete Comparative Guide

🕐

Prayer Times — When to Pray

Sunni (All Schools)

Five prayers at five separate times: Fajr (pre-dawn), Zuhr (midday), Asr (afternoon), Maghrib (immediately after sunset), Isha (night). Each prayer has its own prescribed window with a beginning and end time. Combining prayers is only permitted during travel or illness in most Sunni schools.

Shia (Jafari)

The five prayers are permissibly combined into three sessions: Fajr alone; Zuhr + Asr back-to-back (Jama'); Maghrib + Isha back-to-back. All five obligatory prayers are prayed, but combining them is always permitted — not just for travellers. Basis: the Prophet himself combined prayers in Medina without apparent reason, per Sahih Muslim.

🤲

Hand Position While Standing (Qiyam)

Sunni

Hands folded — right over left. Placement varies by school: below the navel (Hanafi school), at the navel, or on the chest (Shafi'i / Hanbali). Folding (qabd) is considered established Sunnah by all four schools based on multiple hadiths.

Shia (Jafari)

Hands kept at the sides throughout the standing position (sadl). No folding. Shia scholars argue folding was not practiced during the Prophet's time and was introduced later. Importantly, the Maliki school of Sunni jurisprudence also recommends this position — evidence the difference is not absolute.

🌿

Prostration Surface (Sajda) — The Turbah

Sunni

The forehead touches the floor or prayer mat directly. A carpet, rug, or any clean, stable surface is permissible. No special stone or tablet is required. The Prophet prayed on various surfaces including sand, palm-leaf mats, and bare earth.

Shia (Jafari)

A small clay tablet (turbah) — often made from the soil of Karbala — is placed for the forehead to rest on. Basis: the Prophet commanded prostration "on the earth" (al-ard), and man-made carpet is not earth. The Karbala soil carries additional spiritual weight as the resting place of Imam Husayn. Sunan al-Bayhaqi

📢

The Adhan — Call to Prayer

Sunni

The Fajr (dawn) adhan adds: "Al-salatu khayrun minan-nawm" — "Prayer is better than sleep." The standard adhan is attributed to Bilal ibn Rabah, designated by the Prophet. The formula ends with "Lā ilāha illallāh."

Shia (Jafari)

The phrase "Hayya ala khayr al-amal" (Come to the best of deeds) is added after "Hayya ala al-falah." The "nawm" (sleep) phrase used in Fajr is omitted — Shia scholars argue it was introduced by Caliph Umar, not during the Prophet's time. This is a deliberate theological reversal of what they see as a post-Prophetic innovation (bid'ah).

🗣️

Saying "Ameen" After Surah al-Fatiha

Sunni

Saying Ameen after the imam recites Surah al-Fatiha is considered wajib (obligatory) in Shafi'i and Hanbali schools, and Sunnah in Hanafi. It is spoken aloud in some schools (Shafi'i), silently in others (Hanafi). Multiple hadiths support the practice.

Shia (Jafari)

Shia jurisprudence prohibits saying Ameen during prayer — scholars cite that Ameen is of Hebrew origin and was not part of the original Prophetic practice as transmitted through the Imams. Saying it is considered an innovation (bid'ah) that invalidates the prayer entirely.

💧

Wudu (Ablution) — Treating the Feet

Sunni

Feet are washed up to the ankles during wudu. This is the agreed position of all four Sunni schools and is considered the clear meaning of Surah al-Maidah 5:6. Wiping is only permitted over special leather socks (khuffs).

Shia (Jafari)

Feet are wiped (mash), not washed. The Shia reading of Surah al-Maidah 5:6 notes that the Arabic grammar ("wa arjulakum") is genuinely ambiguous — it could join the wiping clause grammatically. Imam Jafar al-Sadiq explicitly taught wiping. Surah 5:6 (Quran)

🦶

Sitting Position During Tashahhud

Sunni

Sitting on the inner right foot (turned on its side with toes pointing right), with the left foot flat beneath — a position called iftirash. The right index finger is raised and sometimes moved while reciting the shahada. This posture has a specific hadith basis in Abu Dawud and Tirmidhi.

Shia (Jafari)

Sitting with both feet folded naturally beneath the body (tawarruk). A simpler, more symmetrical posture. Hands rest flat on the thighs — no raised index finger. The simplicity is itself considered evidence that this was the original practice, uncomplicated by later additions.

The critical shared foundation: Despite all these differences, Sunni and Shia Muslims can and do pray in the same mosque. During the Hajj in Mecca, Muslims of both traditions pray side by side — a yearly reminder of their shared foundation: the Quran, the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the five pillars, and the fundamental declaration: "Lā ilāha illallāh, Muḥammadun rasūlullāh." There is no god but Allah, and Muhammad is His Messenger.

Part Seven

Analysis:
Who Was Right?

This is historical and logical analysis — not a religious verdict, and not the position of any authority. But the evidence, evaluated carefully and without partisanship, points in specific directions on specific questions.

Evidence Supporting the Shia Position on Ali's Right

The exclusion of Ali from the Saqifa meeting was clearly deliberate: the Prophet's family was occupied with his burial, and succession was decided without their knowledge or consent. That Umar ibn al-Khattab himself called the event a falta — in the Sunni hadith corpus, in Sahih al-Bukhari — is not the language of an orderly, divinely sanctioned transition. It is the language of a man who knew it was rushed.

The Ghadir Khumm event is historically unimpeachable. The mutawatir status of the hadith means both traditions accept it occurred. When the Prophet asked "Do I not have more authority over you than yourselves?" before the declaration, the sequential logic strongly implies that mawla means something beyond friendship. The reading that it means only "friend" requires explaining why the Prophet chose such a grammatically weighted term and such a dramatic occasion merely to express affection for a companion.

Fatima al-Zahra's death — estranged from the ruling government, refusing to allow the first Caliph to attend her funeral, dying in grief over her inheritance — is morally catastrophic for the Sunni narrative. If Abu Bakr was the rightfully chosen, divinely acceptable Caliph, why did the Prophet's daughter die in this condition? The Sunni narrative has no fully satisfying answer. Neither tradition disputes the basic facts.

Muawiya's open recantation of the Hasan treaty — his own words, preserved in al-Tabari — and the massacre at Karbala are not contested history. Whatever one thinks of the Saqifa decision, the Umayyad caliphate that resulted was, by the standards of its own Islamic tradition, morally catastrophic. The public cursing of Ali from mosque pulpits for 60 years is historical record.

Evidence Supporting the Sunni Position on Pragmatic Legitimacy

Ali's eventual pledge of allegiance — however reluctant, however delayed — and his decades of service as chief legal scholar under Abu Bakr, Umar, and Uthman means that the early caliphate functioned with his at least partial participation. He did not take up arms against the first three Caliphs. That implicit cooperation was noticed by the historians of the time, and it was Ali himself who recognised the pragmatic necessity of Muslim unity.

The early Muslim state — the Ridda wars, the administrative innovations, the Quran's compilation, the extraordinary conquests — happened under Abu Bakr and Umar. Whatever the moral questions at the origin, the institution worked. Islam became a global civilisation within a century of the Prophet's death precisely because of the continuity the Sunni caliphate provided. The Sunni model was not simply an act of usurpation — it was also a functional government that preserved the faith.

The Sunni model of communal consultation (shura) created a theoretically correctable political structure. It acknowledged human fallibility and prevented the total concentration of religious and political authority in a single hereditary line. When that line failed catastrophically — as Yazid demonstrated — the Sunni model had at least theoretical institutional mechanisms for correction. The caliphate of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz (717–720 CE), who abolished the cursing of Ali and restored equity, is cited as evidence the system could reform itself.

The Shia-Sunni divide is not a disagreement between two groups who misunderstand each other. It is a disagreement between two groups who understand the same events very differently — and who both have serious, historically grounded reasons for their position.

The honest conclusion — reached after evaluating primary sources from both traditions
Historical Perspective

What 1,400 Years of Distance Tells Us

The events of 632–680 CE were not abstract theological disputes. They were real people — the companions of the Prophet, his family, his daughter — navigating genuinely unprecedented circumstances with no rulebook. Abu Bakr, Umar, and Uthman were not villains in any simple sense. Ali, Hasan, and Husayn were not helpless victims.

What happened was a collision of two legitimate principles: the pragmatic need for immediate, stable leadership, and the principle that succession in a theocentric community should not be a purely political act. Both principles had merit. Neither fully prevailed.

What Karbala did — permanently, irreversibly — was to move that collision from the domain of political history into the domain of cosmic meaning. The death of Husayn became not merely a historical event but an enduring wound, a moral indictment, and for hundreds of millions of people across fourteen centuries, a living call to conscience. Every year, on the plains outside a small Iraqi city called Karbala, that call is answered by the largest human gathering on earth.
